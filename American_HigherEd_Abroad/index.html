About the Book

In a world where political and cultural divisions seem increasingly entrenched and global challenges demand collaborative solutions, universities remain one of the few spaces where people from different cultures, traditions, and worldviews can live and learn together. American Higher Education Abroad: Lessons in Culture, Curriculum, and Community examines how American-inspired models of the university are adapted—sometimes successfully, sometimes imperfectly—within diverse global contexts.

As centrifugal forces separate countries and cultures, this monograph explores both the promise and the friction inherent in building campuses that bring together a wide spectrum of beliefs, nationalities, and identities. Through the lens of leading global institutions, the book considers what happens when educational ideas rooted in the United States encounter established local traditions, regulatory environments, and cultural norms. It highlights the necessity of translation, partnership, and deep listening, and argues that no truly diverse community can flourish through the simple export of a single institutional culture.

Rather than presenting American higher education as a universal template, the volume reflects on how global campuses evolve through negotiation and adaptation—becoming laboratories for intercultural dialogue, shared leadership, and mutual learning across borders.

Book Parts & Chapters

Introduction

The Idea of the Global American University and Fostering Cosmopolitanism and Global Citizenship in the 21st Century

by Bryan Penprase (SUA)

Part I: Theoretical Foundations & Visionary Ideals

▼

Part I of the book explores the theoretical foundations and institutional ambitions behind a set of global campuses shaped by American-inspired educational models. It examines the idea of a global education that encourages students to appreciate their own cultural heritage while placing that heritage within a broader international context.

This section engages the literature of cosmopolitanism and global citizenship, offering frameworks for articulating the value of global education to students, faculty, staff, and the broader public. At the same time, it considers how these ideals are interpreted and adapted across different cultural and political settings, recognizing that global education is not simply exported but reshaped through dialogue, negotiation, and local context.

Part II: Curriculum, Pedagogy & Practice

▼

Part II examines the curricular and co-curricular innovations designed to facilitate multicultural understanding within the classroom and beyond. It provides a scaffold for addressing multicultural issues, especially as they relate to international students’ diverse educational assumptions and expectations.

The book includes materials for intercultural teaching, guides for structuring discussion and collaboration within a global classroom, and approaches for surfacing and leveraging the strengths of a diverse and international student body. At the same time, it reflects on the limits of any single pedagogical model, recognizing that teaching practices rooted in one academic culture must often be translated and adapted in another.

Amid rising international student populations worldwide, the lessons from these global campuses offer insights into enhancing student learning, engagement, retention, and success—while acknowledging that effective multicultural pedagogy emerges through dialogue, experimentation, and responsiveness to local context.

Part III: Student Experiences, Community & Belonging

▼

Part III explores on-the-ground student experiences of residential colleges, the nuanced process of hiring and partnering with local staff, and the complexities of diversity, equity, and inclusion initiatives in a global context. It considers how assumptions about identity, authority, inclusion, and community shift when students and staff from dozens of cultural traditions share a campus.

A central focus is on experiential and peer-to-peer learning as indispensable tools for cultivating belonging and shared purpose. Through case studies of orientations, town halls, peer mentorship programs, and the active involvement of student leaders, this section brings to life the vibrancy—and occasional friction—of campus life. It highlights the importance of listening across difference and amplifying voices from across the student body, particularly those whose perspectives may not align neatly with imported frameworks of diversity and inclusion.

About the Editors

Dr. Bryan Edward Penprase

Vice President for Sponsored Research and External Academic Relations and Professor of Astronomy, Soka University of America

Bryan E. Penprase is a scholar of higher education, technology, and innovation with extensive experience in global education initiatives. He is the author of multiple books on higher education transformation and frequently speaks at international conferences on educational innovation, technology integration, and the future of learning.

Dr. Kyle Farley

Partner, Cross Borders Education

Kyle Farley brings extensive expertise in global higher education initiatives and cross-cultural educational leadership. With experience across multiple global campuses, he contributes deep insights into building effective international learning communities.

Sara Pervaiz Amjad

Associate Dean of Student Affairs, Director of Student Life and Spiritual Life, Intercultural Education, & Conflict Transformation (SLICCET), NYU Abu Dhabi

Sara Pervaiz Amjad leads efforts in fostering inclusive communities and intercultural dialogue at one of the world's leading global universities. Her work focuses on creating belonging and addressing the complexities of diversity, equity, and inclusion in global educational contexts.
